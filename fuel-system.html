Aircraft Fuel System Architecture

Conceptual fuel system architecture for an A320-class aircraft, focused on safe storage, transfer, venting, inerting, and water management under certification-led constraints.

Read case study Back to projects
Focus
Fuel storage, venting, inerting, water drainage
Context
Airbus-supported Cranfield group project
Methods
CS-25 review, architecture definition, safety thinking

Problem

Design a fuel system concept that keeps the aircraft supplied across operating conditions while controlling tank pressure, reducing ignition risk, and managing water contamination.

Detailed design data is confidential, so this page presents the high-level engineering approach and decision areas.

Approach

  • Defined the fuel storage and transfer architecture for a transport aircraft concept.
  • Mapped venting needs to tank pressure control and safe operating envelopes.
  • Integrated an inerting concept using OBIGGS-style oxygen reduction logic.
  • Built a water drainage and contamination control strategy around aircraft serviceability.
  • Kept certification expectations visible while shaping subsystem decisions.

Engineering Work

System Architecture

Fuel tank configuration, piping logic, equipment placement, and subsystem interaction.

Venting and Pressure

Pressure equilibrium logic for tank integrity across climb, cruise, descent, and refuelling cases.

Inerting System

Reduced flammability risk through oxygen concentration management and inert gas distribution.

Safety and Certification

Design choices framed around CS-25 expectations, redundancy, maintainability, and failure tolerance.

Visual Space

Insight

Fuel system design is safety-critical because storage, transfer, venting, inerting, and drainage decisions affect each other. The design trade is not only technical performance, but also weight, redundancy, maintainability, and certifiability.

Next Steps

  • Develop a detailed FMEA and fault tree for the fuel system functions.
  • Model transient fuel behaviour, water accumulation, and vent pressure response.
  • Convert the concept architecture into a component-level validation plan.
