Aircraft Fuel System Architecture Design

Airbus-supported Group Design Project | Cranfield University

Due to project confidentiality, detailed schematics and numerical data are omitted. This page focuses on system-level design methodology and engineering decisions.

1. Overview

This project involved the conceptual design of a complete aircraft fuel system for a next-generation commercial aircraft operating on Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF). The objective was to ensure continuous fuel supply, safe operation, and compliance with CS-25 certification requirements.

2. System Scope

  • Fuel storage (wing and centre tanks)
  • Fuel transfer and engine feed
  • Refuel / defuel systems
  • Fuel jettison capability
  • Fuel tank venting
  • Inerting system (OBIGGS)
  • Water management

3. System Architecture

The final configuration included a central tank and wing tanks with controlled fuel transfer for centre of gravity management. Multiple architectures were evaluated, including gravity-fed and pump-driven systems, with final selection prioritising simplicity, reliability, and safety.

4. My Contribution

Fuel Tank Venting

Designed venting system to maintain pressure equilibrium during climb, descent, and refuelling. Included vent lines, surge tank, and pressure relief mechanisms.

Inerting System

Evaluated OBIGGS and GOBIGGS architectures. Selected OBIGGS based on maturity, integration feasibility, and certification readiness.

Water Management

Analysed water accumulation and removal strategies considering SAF properties, focusing on reliability and maintenance implications.

Trade Studies

Contributed to architecture-level decisions, balancing complexity, weight, safety, and maintainability.

5. Engineering Approach

  • Requirements-driven system design
  • Functional Hazard Analysis (FHA)
  • V-model systems engineering approach
  • Iterative architecture development

6. Safety & Certification

The system was designed in alignment with EASA CS-25 requirements, ensuring:

  • Continuous fuel supply under all conditions
  • Independent engine feed systems
  • Safe tank pressure management
  • Mitigation of ignition risks

7. Key Engineering Trade-Offs

  • Simplicity vs redundancy
  • Weight vs safety systems
  • Innovation vs certification risk
  • Maintenance vs performance

8. Tools & Methods

  • Systems engineering (V-model)
  • Preliminary sizing calculations
  • Trade studies and safety analysis
  • CAD exposure (CATIA)

9. Outcome

Delivered a complete conceptual fuel system architecture with integrated safety subsystems, aligned with certification requirements and supported by engineering analysis and trade studies.
