A320 Wing Load Distribution Analysis

Preliminary Design | Structural Analysis | Computational Modelling

Developed a structural model of a tapered transport aircraft wing to evaluate how aerodynamic loading translates into internal loads and stress distribution along the span.

Tools: MATLAB | Numerical Integration (trapz) | Beam Theory

Problem

Determine the structural response of a semi-span A320-class wing under aerodynamic loading, including shear force, bending moment, and bending stress distribution, and assess structural safety under simplified flight conditions.

Approach

  • Applied elliptical lift distribution representing efficient aerodynamic loading
  • Discretised wing span into 100 stations
  • Computed shear force and bending moment using numerical integration (trapz)
  • Modelled spanwise-varying geometry based on chord scaling
  • Evaluated second moment of area along span
  • Calculated bending stress using Euler–Bernoulli beam theory

Results

Max Moment

~2.2 MN·m

Max Stress

~88 MPa

Yield Stress

300 MPa

FoS

~3.4

Analysis Visualisation

Engineering Insight

Peak bending stress occurs at the wing root due to maximum bending moment. Stress decreases rapidly towards the tip as internal loads reduce and taper improves structural efficiency. This reflects real aircraft design practice, where material is concentrated near the root to carry primary loads.

Key Design Drivers

  • Thickness-to-chord ratio strongly affects stiffness (I ∝ h³)
  • Spar width influences stiffness linearly but is less dominant
  • Load magnitude directly scales structural stress
  • Taper ratio improves load distribution and reduces outboard stress

Engineering Assessment

The calculated factor of safety (~3.4) indicates structural adequacy under steady flight loading. However, real aircraft structures must satisfy certification requirements such as CS-25 limit and ultimate loads (e.g. 2.5g manoeuvres).

Under such conditions, stresses would increase significantly, reducing the margin of safety. This highlights the need for higher-fidelity modelling and realistic load cases in detailed design stages.

Limitations

  • No torsional or aeroelastic effects considered
  • Simplified single-spar structural model
  • No discrete loads (engine, fuel, structural weight)
  • Linear material behaviour assumed

Next Steps

  • Develop full wing box model (front & rear spars + skin)
  • Include torsional stiffness and aeroelastic effects
  • Apply certification load cases (CS-25)
  • Validate results using finite element analysis (FEA)
